Physics overview

Why the Titanic Sank

A ship floats when its overall average density is less than the density of water. The Titanic did not sink simply because it "got heavy". It sank because flooding increased the ship’s total mass while its external volume stayed almost the same, so its average density rose until it became greater than that of seawater.

The big idea

Steel is denser than water, but a ship is mostly hollow. That hollow volume means the ship as a whole has a much lower average density than solid steel. As long as the ship plus the air inside has an average density below the density of water, it can float.

average density = total mass / total volume

Before the collision, the Titanic’s total mass was spread over a huge volume that included large air-filled spaces. After the hull was damaged, seawater rushed into those spaces. The mass of the ship increased dramatically, but the outside shape and volume of the ship barely changed at first. That pushed the average density upward.

The key physics statement is this: once the ship’s overall average density became greater than the density of the surrounding water, floating was no longer possible.

What changed after the iceberg impact?

1. The hull was breached Water entered compartments that were supposed to stay dry.
2. Air space was lost Empty volume that helped keep the average density low was replaced by water.
3. Mass increased The ship now included the mass of the incoming seawater.
4. Average density rose Same rough external volume, but much greater total mass.
5. The ship sank lower More of the ship had to go below the waterline to balance its weight.
6. Flooding worsened As the bow dropped, more water entered, creating a runaway effect.

A density-based way to picture it

Imagine two sealed containers of the same outside size:

  • One is mostly full of air and a little metal: it can float.
  • The other is filled with water and metal: its average density is much higher.

The Titanic effectively moved from the first situation toward the second as compartments flooded. The more water entered the hull, the less the ship behaved like a giant hollow object and the more it behaved like a very dense body.

Low average density → floats
High average density → sinks

Simple visual model

The outside volume changes little at first, but the mass rises as internal air spaces fill with water.

Why “mass displacement” can confuse students

Students often hear that a floating object “displaces its own weight in water.” That is true, but it can become a bit abstract. The density explanation is often more intuitive:

  • A floating ship must have a low enough overall average density.
  • Flooding increases the mass without giving the ship useful extra volume.
  • That means the average density increases.
  • Once it exceeds the density of water, the ship cannot remain afloat.
So the best classroom phrasing is not “the Titanic sank because it became too heavy” but “the Titanic sank because flooding increased its average density above that of the surrounding water.”

In one sentence

The Titanic floated while its total mass was low enough for its very large volume, but once flooding filled enough of the ship with water, its overall average density became greater than that of seawater, so it could no longer float.
